Choco + Leticia.

Leticia

ChocoLet started in a two-burner kitchen in Rio, with a recipe that belonged to Leticia's grandmother before it belonged to anyone with a storefront.

She'd been rolling truffles for family for a decade before she sold a single one — birthdays, holidays, the odd wedding favor. The name was never a branding exercise. It's just what people already called the boxes she brought over: "the ChocoLet ones."

In 2022 she rented a commercial kitchen two mornings a week. Today it's five, still hand-rolled, still tasted one by one before anything ships — not because a machine couldn't do it faster, but because that's the part she never wanted to give up.

What we won't compromise

Three things that don't change no matter how big this gets.

Direct trade

We buy cacao directly from two farms we visit in person, at a price set above commodity market rate — not through a broker.

No preservatives

Every truffle is meant to be eaten within ten days. If it lasted longer, something would be added that doesn't belong there.

Hand-rolled, always

We've turned down wholesale orders that would've required a rolling machine. That's the line we won't cross.

Taste it yourself

The story's nice. The truffles are the point.
